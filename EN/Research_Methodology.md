# Methodological Design of Marketing Research: Testing the "Helpa" Brand for a Digital Lending Service in the Indian Market

## Project Summary

An international fintech company is preparing to enter the Indian digital lending market under the **Helpa** brand. Before launch, it is necessary to validate brand perception among real target borrowers in India.

**Key research objectives:**
- Go / no-go decision on using the brand in India
- Identifying risks (trust, associations, cultural pitfalls)
- Recommendations for brand refinement (tone, promise, positioning)

**Testing subjects:**
- Brand name "Helpa" (blind and in context)
- App interface prototypes (key screens)
- Google Play page mockup (icon, screenshots, description)

All visual stimuli are provided by the client and are used during sessions to assess brand perception, trust, and willingness to install the app.

**Total sample:** 70-130 people. **Methods:** focus groups (1-2), in-depth interviews (15-20), chatbot-administered surveys (50-100).

Below is the methodological architecture ensuring the scientific rigor of each step.

---

## 1. General Approach: Mixed Methods

**What it is.** We simultaneously collect qualitative data (focus groups, interviews) and quantitative data (surveys), then compare the results. This is called a **convergent parallel design**.

**Why this approach.** Qualitative methods provide depth — we understand *why* people react in a certain way. Quantitative methods provide breadth — we see *how often* certain reactions occur. Comparison increases the reliability of conclusions through triangulation.

**As applied to this project:** focus groups reveal group dynamics of trust in the brand and cultural patterns; in-depth interviews uncover individual fears, negative experiences with lending apps, and decision-making mechanisms for "install / don't install"; surveys quantitatively capture the distribution of associations, trust levels, and willingness to install.

**Key sources:**
- Creswell, J.W. & Plano Clark, V.L. (2018). *Designing and Conducting Mixed Methods Research*, 3rd ed., SAGE. — [SAGE Edition](https://collegepublishing.sagepub.com/products/designing-and-conducting-mixed-methods-research-3-241842)
- Creswell, J.W. (2025). A process for conducting mixed methods data analysis. *Journal of General and Family Medicine*. — [Wiley Article](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/jgf2.736)

## 2. Target Audience (Strict Recruitment Criteria)

Deviation from the profile is unacceptable. Respondents must fully meet all specified criteria.

### 2.1. Demographics and Employment

| Parameter | Requirement |
|-----------|-------------|
| Age | 22-40 years (focus: 24-35) |
| Gender | Any (proportions are not equalized — we reflect the actual borrower mix) |
| Location | Tier-1 and Tier-2 cities: Delhi NCR, Mumbai, Bengaluru, Hyderabad, Pune, Chennai, Ahmedabad, Jaipur, Indore, Lucknow |
| Employment | Salaried employees (private sector), self-employed / gig workers with regular income |
| Income (net) | INR 15,000 - 50,000/month (focus: INR 20,000 - 35,000) |

**Excluded:** students without income, unemployed, retirees.

### 2.2. Financial and Credit Profile (Critical Filter)

- Thin credit file (thin-file), near-prime or sub-prime
- Limited or no access to bank credit cards / personal loans
- At least one of the following experiences: bank loan rejection, long approval wait, requirement for a guarantor / excessive documentation
- Within the last 12 months **received at least one** digital personal loan through a mobile app or NBFC platform
- Typical loan amount: INR 3,000 - 50,000, term 7-90 days

### 2.3. Digital Behavior (Mandatory)

- Android smartphone (>= 95% of sample)
- Daily mobile internet usage
- Comfortable installing and using financial apps
- Has Aadhaar, completed at least one digital KYC (Aadhaar / PAN / selfie / bank account)
- Uses UPI (mandatory) and bank account (mandatory)

### 2.4. Hard Exclusions

The following respondents **are not included in any method:**
- Bank prime borrowers with active credit cards
- HNI / upper middle class
- Rural population
- Users who have never taken a digital loan
- Respondents from generic panels without borrowing experience verification

### 2.5. Borrower Experience Validation (Deal-Breaker)

Borrowing experience is verified, not taken at face value. Verification through:
- App recognition (showing screenshots / icons of lending apps)
- Description of the loan application process (flow)
- Recollection of repayment experience

Samples based on general population panels, social media recruitment, or student databases **are not permitted**.

## 3. Sample Size Justification N = 70-130

### 3.1. For the Qualitative Component (Interviews, Focus Groups)

Sample size is determined not by statistics, but by **information power** — the ability of collected data to answer the research question.

The **information power model** by Malterud et al. (2016) evaluates five factors: narrowness of aim, specificity of sample, reliance on theory, quality of dialogue, analysis strategy. The higher each factor, the fewer people are needed. Our aim is narrow (a specific brand for a specific product), the sample is highly specific (verified digital loan borrowers), the theory is established (Hofstede, Keller, GLOBE) — all of this justifies a compact sample.

**Empirical benchmarks:**
- Guest, Bunce & Johnson (2006) showed that core themes are identified with as few as **6-12 interviews**. — [Article](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1525822X05279903)
- Guest, Namey & McKenna (2017): **80% of themes** in focus groups are discovered within **2-3 sessions**. — [Article](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1525822X16639015)
- Malterud, K. et al. (2016). Sample Size in Qualitative Interview Studies. *Qualitative Health Research*, 26(13). — [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26613970/)

### 3.2. For the Quantitative Component (Surveys, 50-100 People)

With N = 50-100 we are in a zone where meaningful statistical analysis is already possible. With 50 respondents, **large effects** (Cohen's d >= 0.80) are confidently detected. With 100, **medium effects** (d >= 0.50) also become detectable, which significantly expands analytical capabilities. It is recommended to use nonparametric tests (Mann-Whitney U, chi-squared), confidence intervals, and effect sizes — they work correctly at these volumes and do not require normality assumptions.

### 3.3. Sample Structure

| Method | N | Role in Research |
|--------|---|------------------|
| Focus groups (1-2 x 5-6 people) | 5-12 | Group dynamics of trust, cultural norms, first reactions to the brand, competitor discussion |
| In-depth interviews (IDI) | 15-20 | Individual perception, trust and fear drivers, brand rejection signals, sensitive experiences with lenders |
| Survey (chatbot) | 50-100 | Quantitative support for qualitative findings, association ranking, trust scales, statistical analysis |

**Total sample: 70-130 people.** The exact number within the range is determined during the fieldwork phase — the qualitative part is regulated by the saturation principle, the quantitative part — by respondent availability and required analytical depth.

**Sampling strategy** — purposive sampling per Patton (2015): criterion sampling (verified digital loan users matching the full target audience profile) + maximum variation (diversity by income, city, presence / absence of negative experience with lenders).

- Patton, M.Q. (2015). *Qualitative Research & Evaluation Methods*, 4th ed., SAGE. — [SAGE Edition](https://collegepublishing.sagepub.com/products/qualitative-research-evaluation-methods-4-232962)

## 4. Detailed Method Descriptions

### 4.1. Focus Groups (1-2 Sessions)

**Format:** online or in-person, 5-6 participants per group.

**Group segmentation logic.** If 2 groups are conducted, segmentation is based on one of the following criteria (priority determined during recruitment):
- Income group: INR 15-25K vs. INR 25-50K
- Experience with lenders: negative (rejection, harassment, hidden fees) vs. neutral
- City tier: Tier-1 vs. Tier-2

**Moderation:**
- The moderator must have experience in financial services / lending research
- Sessions are conducted in the local language (Hindi and/or regional language), results are summarized in English
- Mandatory principle: **actively probe negative experiences** with lending apps; superficial "looks fine" responses are not accepted (direct client requirement)

**Key stimuli:** the name "Helpa" (blind -> in context), UI prototypes, Google Play page mockup.

**Duration:** 60-90 minutes.

**Recording:** video recording of all sessions (delivered to the client).

### 4.2. In-Depth Interviews — IDI (15-20)

**Format:** one-on-one, online (video call) or in-person. Duration — 40-60 minutes.

**Why IDI is the primary qualitative method in this project.** The client brief directly states that IDI are particularly useful for:
- Trust and fear drivers
- Brand rejection signals
- Sensitive experiences with lenders (harassment, data leaks, hidden penalties)

These topics are associated with shame and fear — respondents open up significantly better in individual conversations than in groups.

**Recruitment and segmentation.** Within the 15-20 interviews, maximum variation is ensured:
- By income (lower and upper bounds of the range)
- By city (minimum 3-4 different cities from the target audience list)
- By gender (reflecting the actual mix)
- By experience: some respondents — with negative experience (bank rejection, collector harassment), some — with neutral experience

**Moderation:** moderator with experience in financial research. Indirect questioning techniques (projective methods, third-person tasks) to bypass social desirability — see Section 6.3 (Erin Meyer).

### 4.3. Chatbot-Administered Survey (50-100)

**Format:** structured survey administered via chatbot (e.g., Telegram / WhatsApp bot).

**Why a chatbot.** The target audience consists of mobile users with daily messenger usage. A chatbot provides:
- High response rate (familiar environment, no new app installation required)
- Ability to embed visual stimuli (logo, UI screenshots, Google Play mockup) directly into the survey flow
- Controlled step-by-step progression through blocks (brand -> UI -> Google Play -> summary)
- Logic branching and skip logic
- Automatic data capture

**Screening and validation.** Before granting access to the main survey, the chatbot conducts screening:
1. Demographic filters (age, city, income, employment)
2. Digital lending experience validation through indirect checks:
   - Recognition of icons / interfaces of real lending apps
   - Description of the loan application sequence (from a set of options)
   - Questions about repayment experience

Respondents who fail the screening are not admitted to the survey and are not compensated.

**Compensation.** Respondents who fully complete the survey and meet all target audience criteria (based on indirect indicators) receive monetary compensation. The compensation amount is lower than for IDI or focus group participation, corresponding to the lower time commitment.

**Survey content** (block structure — see Section 8):
- Semantic differential scales (trust, friendliness, legitimacy)
- Association ranking
- Brand memorability tasks
- Willingness to install rating (Likert scale)
- Open-ended questions (limited number — 3-4 for the entire survey)

## 6. Cross-Cultural Frameworks: Understanding the Indian User

Culture affects everything: from how a person perceives an interface to how they relate to debt. We use three models for three-dimensional analysis.

### 6.1. Hofstede's Cultural Dimensions (6 Dimensions)

**What it does.** Compares countries across six scales: power distance, individualism/collectivism, masculinity/femininity, uncertainty avoidance, long-term orientation, indulgence.

**What matters for us.** India has high power distance (trust in authorities, institutions) with moderate uncertainty avoidance. For the brand, this means: partnerships with NBFCs and institutional trust signals (RBI licenses, partner logos) function as trust signals more powerfully than detailed fine print. This directly influences which UI elements we test first.

- Hofstede, G. et al. (2010). *Cultures and Organizations*, 3rd ed., McGraw-Hill. — [Hofstede Insights](https://www.theculturefactor.com/frequently-asked-questions)

### 6.2. GLOBE Study (9 Dimensions, Financial Sector Focus)

**What it does.** A large-scale study (17,300 managers, 62 countries) that extends Hofstede to 9 dimensions and separates "as is" from "as should be." One of the three target industries is **financial services**, making GLOBE directly applicable.

**Why we need it.** GLOBE provides financial sector specificity that Hofstede lacks. India belongs to the "South Asia" cluster — we use ready-made cluster data to interpret respondent reactions to institutional signals in the app.

- House, R.J. et al. (2004). *Culture, Leadership, and Organizations: The GLOBE Study of 62 Societies*, Sage. — [GLOBE Project](https://globeproject.com/study_2004_2007)

### 6.3. Erin Meyer's Culture Map (8 Scales)

**What it does.** A practical model of 8 behavioral scales: communication style, decision-making, trust-building, attitude toward time, etc.

**Why we need it.** Meyer is critically important when designing guides for focus groups and interviews. Indian respondents avoid direct confrontation and often give socially desirable answers — especially in the context of financial topics associated with stigma. This means that:
- Questions are formulated indirectly: not "do you like this design?" but "show me which option you would choose for a friend"
- Projective techniques are mandatory (Section 8.1): "imagine your colleague is thinking about installing this app — what would you tell them?"
- The moderator must actively probe beyond "looks fine" (direct client requirement)

- Meyer, E. (2014). *The Culture Map*, PublicAffairs. — [ErinMeyer.com](https://erinmeyer.com/corporate-mapping-tool-report/)

## 7. Brand and Naming Testing

### 7.1. Keller's Brand Equity Pyramid (CBBE)

**What it does.** Describes how consumers build a relationship with a brand through four levels: awareness -> meaning (associations, characteristics) -> judgments and feelings -> resonance (loyalty).

**Why we need it.** The Keller pyramid structures the survey itself and the discussion guides. Levels are linked to specific research blocks:
- **Identity (awareness):** "Brand blind" block (Section 8.1) — what is this app? What does it do?
- **Meaning (associations):** "Brand in context" block (Section 8.1) — is the name suitable for a lending service?
- **Response (judgments and feelings):** UI and Google Play blocks (Section 8.2, Section 8.3) — I trust / don't trust, I would install / wouldn't install
- **Resonance:** "Final verdict" block (Section 8.6) — I would recommend / wouldn't recommend

- Keller, K.L. & Swaminathan, V. (2020). *Strategic Brand Management*, 5th ed., Pearson. — [DAU](https://www.dau.edu/artifact/kellers-brand-equity-model)

### 7.2. Aaker's Brand Equity + 5Bs (2025)

**What it does.** Measures brand strength through five components. The current 2025 model — 5Bs: Brand Equity, Relevance, Image, Loyalty, Portfolio.

**Why we need it.** Aaker enables benchmarking — we compare the perception of "Helpa" with the perception of competitors (RupeeRedee, NIRA, CASHe, mPokket, etc.) along the same axes. Benchmarking is conducted in focus groups (qualitatively) and surveys (quantitatively — rating scales).

- Aaker, D.A. (2025). The 5Bs of Modern Branding. *California Management Review*, Sage. — [Sage Journals](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/2694104X251349027)

### 7.3. Brand Linguistics — Testing the Sound of the Name

**What it does.** Evaluates how a brand's phonetics are perceived across different languages. Research shows that sounds carry cross-cultural meanings: front vowels are associated with "small" and "light," back vowels — with "large" and "heavy."

**Why we need it.** "Helpa" is a name that is not a word in either Hindi or English (in standard spelling). It is necessary to test:
- Phonetic associations in Hindi and regional languages (Tamil, Telugu, Marathi, Kannada — depending on recruitment cities)
- Pronunciation: uniformity or variability
- Memorability: recall 15-20 minutes after first exposure
- Unintended sound similarities with negative words in local languages

- Carnevale, M. et al. (2017). Brand Linguistics. *International Journal of Research in Marketing*. — [NEIU Research](https://neiudc.neiu.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1000&context=uhp-projects)

## 8. Discussion Guide Structure (Key Blocks)

The following blocks are used across all three methods with corresponding adaptations: focus groups — discussion format, IDI — in-depth individual conversation, survey (chatbot) — closed-ended questions with scales and limited open-ended fields.

### 8.1. "Brand" Block — Blind and in Context

**Stage 1: without explanation.** The respondent is shown only the name "Helpa" (without explanation or context).
- What is this app?
- What does it help with?
- Serious or risky?
- Would you click on it in Google Play? Why / why not?

**Stage 2: after explanation** that this is a lending app.
- Is the name suitable for a money lending service?
- Scale: trustworthy <-> suspicious; friendly <-> aggressive; local <-> foreign
- Would you feel comfortable borrowing money from an app with this name?

### 8.2. "UI Prototypes" Block — Trust Signals and Usability

**Focus is on signals, not visual aesthetics** (direct client instruction).
- Does this look like a real financial service?
- What inspires trust? What inspires distrust?
- At what point would you start doubting whether to continue or not?
- What elements increase confidence?
- Are there any "red flags" reminiscent of bad lending apps?

### 8.3. "Google Play Page" Block — Installation Decision

The respondent imagines they are currently looking for a lending app.
- Would you install this app? Why / why not?
- What do you look at first?
- Icon: professional / cheap / fraudulent?
- Do the screenshots increase or decrease trust?
- What would make you immediately close the page?

### 8.4. "Brand-Product Consistency" Block

- Does the app look like something called "Helpa"?
- Is there a disconnect between: the name <-> the interface <-> the store description?
- If the app had a different name, would there be more or less trust?
- Who do you think this app is intended for?

### 8.5. "Conversion and Fear Map" Block

- At which step would you most likely abandon the process?
- What fears arise when looking at: the name / app screens / store page?
- What do you need to see to feel safe enough to submit an application?

**Important (IDI and FG):** the moderator must actively probe past negative experiences with lending apps — collector harassment, data leaks, hidden fees, contact blackmail. Superficial "looks fine" responses are not accepted.

### 8.6. "Final Verdict" Block

- Would you personally use this app?
- Would you recommend it to a friend?
- One word that describes this brand
- One thing you would immediately change

## 9. Cross-Cultural UX Research

### 9.1. Marcus's Cross-Cultural UI Framework

**What it does.** Translates Hofstede's cultural dimensions into specific design parameters: metaphors, navigation, color choices, information density.

**As applied to "Helpa".** High power distance in India -> design should emphasize institutional trust signals (NBFC partner logo, RBI license badges). Collectivism -> social proof elements (reviews, ratings, "X people have already received a loan"). These hypotheses are tested in the UI block (Section 8.2).

- Marcus, A. & Gould, E.W. (2000). Crosscurrents. *Interactions*, 7(4), ACM. — [ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/220383663_Crosscurrents_cultural_dimensions_and_global_Web_user-interface_design)

### 9.2. Indian Digital User Specifics (in the Context of the Target Audience)

When testing UX, it is critical to account for target audience characteristics:
- **Devices:** Android (>= 95% of sample), predominantly budget smartphones -> app size and graphics weight matter for the target audience
- **Payment ecosystem:** UPI is the mandatory payment method for the target audience -> UPI integration should be visible already in prototypes
- **Languages:** Target audience from Tier-1 and Tier-2 cities — most have basic English proficiency, but Hindi and regional languages dominate -> multilingual interface as a trust signal
- **Digital KYC:** all respondents have completed at least one KYC flow -> the KYC interface can be tested for familiarity and comfort

## 11. Market Context for the Researcher

### 11.1. Cultural Stigma of Debt

Inability to repay debt carries significant social stigma in India. The microfinance crisis in Andhra Pradesh (2010) still affects the sector's reputation. This is critical for this research:
- Respondents may conceal or downplay their borrowing experience — indirect validation techniques are necessary
- Brand positioning and communication tone are tested with this sensitivity in mind
- The word "loan" may carry negative connotations — we test alternative frames: "financial help," "salary advance," "instant credit"

### 11.2. Terminology: Absence of "Payday Loan"

The term "payday loan" **does not exist** as an established category in India. The product is described using terms familiar to the local audience — "salary advance," "instant personal loan," "quick cash," etc. Terminology choice is tested within Brand Linguistics (Section 7.3) and the "Brand in Context" block (Section 8.1).

### 11.3. Psychological and Behavioral Profile of the Target Audience (for the Moderator)

It is important for the moderator to understand key behavioral characteristics of the target audience:
- **Financial mindset:** limited cash flow, but regular income exists. Motivation is short-term liquidity, not long-term credit planning
- **Sensitivity to:** approval speed, process simplicity, fee transparency
- **Trust drivers:** App Store rating, clear repayment dates and amounts, perceived legitimacy (NBFC backing, professional UI, absence of "suspicious" signals)
- **Key fears:** collector harassment, personal data leaks, hidden fees and unclear penalties
- **Target audience trust formula:** trust > price. A user is more likely to choose an app they trust than one offering a better rate

## 12. Research Quality Criteria

### 12.1. Lincoln & Guba's Trustworthiness (1985)

Four criteria — the "gold standard" of qualitative research:

| Criterion | Meaning | How We Ensure It |
|-----------|---------|------------------|
| Credibility | Plausibility | Triangulation (comparing data from three sources: FG + IDI + surveys); borrower experience validation |
| Transferability | Applicability | Detailed description of context, demographics, conditions; recording each respondent's city and language |
| Dependability | Reproducibility | Audit trail — documentation of all decisions; video recording of sessions |
| Confirmability | Verifiability | Independent data analysis, researcher reflexivity, verbatim quotes with translation |

- Lincoln, Y.S. & Guba, E.G. (1985). *Naturalistic Inquiry*, Sage. — [Emerald](https://www.emerald.com/qrj/article/23/4/372/359623/Application-of-Guba-and-Lincoln-s-parallel)

### 12.2. Yardley's Criteria (2000)

Four principles: sensitivity to context, rigor, transparency, practical significance. "Sensitivity to context" is particularly valuable — we demonstrate that the research accounts for India's cultural specifics (debt stigma, power distance, social desirability of responses) rather than applying a "Western template."

- Yardley, L. (2000). Dilemmas in qualitative health research. *Psychology and Health*, 15(2). — [ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/247532711_Dilemmas_in_qualitative_research)

### 12.3. Data Analysis Method: Braun & Clarke's Thematic Analysis

A six-phase approach to qualitative data analysis (the most cited method in the field — 400,000+ citations). It enables systematic identification of patterns in respondent answers. As applied to this project: responses are coded along axes from the Keller pyramid (awareness, meaning, response, resonance) and by thematic clusters (trust, fear, memorability, cultural fit).

- Braun, V. & Clarke, V. (2006). Using thematic analysis in psychology. *Qualitative Research in Psychology*, 3(2). — [Taylor & Francis](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1191/1478088706qp063oa)

- Reflects the psychology of real borrowers, not aspirational users

- Provides actionable recommendations (not general observations, but specific steps)
