# Summary: Research Methodology for a Fintech App in the Indian Market

*Based on the document: https://docs.google.com/document/d/1tpMZX94ew85aJld-7YERxRvKucQJ13va77FoSEDOCd0/edit?tab=t.3vkhvffse58e*

## Context

The document describes a comprehensive qualitative research methodology for a foreign fintech brand entering the micro-lending market in India. The target audience consists of so-called "thin-file" borrowers: people with limited access to bank credit who use mobile apps for micro-loans (mPokket, CASHe, KreditBee, etc.). The primary objective is to understand how this segment will perceive a new foreign brand and to identify trust barriers.

## 1. Three-Stage Respondent Verification System

A key market challenge is "professional respondents" who participate en masse in research for the compensation but are not real users. To combat this, a three-stage screening funnel has been proposed.

**Stage 1 — Online Screening Questionnaire** (~10 minutes). The goal is to filter out 60–70% of unsuitable candidates. It includes behavioral trap questions: asking to name specific micro-loan apps, details of the last loan (amount, terms, requested KYC data). Selection criteria are disguised so that respondents cannot guess the "correct" answers. The methodology draws on recommendations from Kantar and DJS Research: behavioral questions yield reliable data, unlike demographic ones. It also includes psychographic agreement-scale questions to identify the borrower's "digital mentality."

**Stage 2 — Phone Interview** (15–20 minutes). In-depth verification through five blocks: warm-up and sincerity check; detailed account of the latest experience (looking for spontaneity, emotions, specific steps); thin-file status verification (checking whether the person has a credit card with a high limit); psychographic profiling; check for "professional respondent" status (participation in more than 2 studies in the past six months is a red flag).

**Stage 3 — Behavioral Verification**. Final filter: requesting screenshots of transactions in Google Pay / PhonePe (with the right to redact personal data) or a descriptive test — a step-by-step account of the application process. A real user reproduces details (OTP, Aadhaar, selfie), while a fake one sticks to generic phrases.

**Final funnel**: out of 1,000 initial candidates — approximately 220 valid respondents (40% pass the questionnaire → 80% pass the interview → 70% pass verification).

## 2. Red Flags and Elimination Criteria

Immediate elimination is applied when the following signs are present: the respondent cannot name a single specific app; gets confused about details when describing their only app; holds a credit card with a limit exceeding 50,000 rupees; works in fintech, banking, or marketing; has participated in more than two studies in the past six months; the account sounds "rehearsed," lacking emotions and details; refuses any form of verification.

## 3. Mystery Browsing — Adapted Brand Testing Methodology

Since the app is not yet available for download, instead of classic mystery shopping, a "mystery browsing" method is used — testing the touchpoints that a user sees before pressing the "Install" button. According to research, these touchpoints determine up to 80% of the download decision.

**Scenario 1 — Google Play Listing Test.** The respondent is shown the app listing and the first 3 seconds of their reaction are recorded: where they look, how they perceive the name (local / foreign / suspicious), what about the icon attracts or repels them. A practical insight from the document: in Tier-2 cities, users instantly "read" a foreign brand by its minimalist icon or "Western" cool colors; icons with warm colors and a rupee symbol receive significantly more clicks.

**Scenario 2 — Static Prototype Navigation.** The respondent "walks through" key screens: registration, permission requests (SMS, contacts, camera), document upload, scoring wait, result. Moments of confusion, fear, frustration, and the desire to close the app are recorded. Example: in one project, it was discovered that requesting SMS access without a prior explanation scared users; adding an explanatory screen increased conversion by 25%.

**Scenario 3 — Comparative Test with Competitors.** Three listings (the new app + two well-known ones, e.g. mPokket and CASHe) are shown simultaneously. Trust rating, specific choice factors, and spontaneous associations are assessed.

## 4. Toolkit and Results Analysis

**Online**: Zoom / Google Meet with screen and face recording, stimulus presentation, collection of spontaneous comments. **Offline**: tablets with prototypes, optional eye-tracking, audio recording. A detailed mystery shopper checklist is also provided, capturing reactions to each screen.

**Quantitative metrics**: share of those who "would install" the app; average trust score (1–10); percentage who flagged "red flags" at each stage; ranking among competitors. **Qualitative insights**: typical fear expressions, cultural associations, moments of trust loss. It is recommended to build a Perception Map with axes "Local — Foreign" and "Trust — Suspicion."

## 5. Summary and Next Steps

The approach makes it possible to obtain valid data on the perception of a foreign brand in the Indian market even without access to a working app. After respondent selection and verification, it is possible to conduct focus groups, mystery browsing, and personal interviews (especially with those who have had negative micro-loan experiences) to gather deep insights.
